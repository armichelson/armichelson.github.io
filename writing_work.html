Home: A Definition Poem

Home (noun) 1. A house, apartment, or other shelter: As in a place where one feels “at home,” safe, comforted, and protected; Her home was Georgia; the state known for its peaches and southern hospitality; the state where people coped with the long, hot summers by drinking tall glasses of sweet tea; people were dependable; days were predictable; She had to make a change; the world around her had gotten so small 2. The place where something is native or most common: True southerners stay in their home state; they bleed red and black; they never venture beyond the borders; they told her she wasn’t going to be happy; she didn’t listen; she was not going to let the people inside it make her decisions. 3. A person’s native place or own country: she loved predictability; she loved to know what was around the next corner; she knew what to expect at home, but how was she going to grow? 4. Any place of residence or refuge: Some say her home is now in another country, and they may be right; her world is no longer so small, and now she is known as the girl who left the state, known as the Big Peach, behind.

Headphones: A Function Poem

One ear, two ear.

With a swift movement I have entered my own sphere.

Tension builds.

Click and play.

The bass kicks in.

The beat drops and I am home.

Each chime and ring awakens a new nerve.

I hold onto each word seeking an escape.

Unwanted outside voices are silenced.

The daily sprint of life is slowed.

Thoughts about life’s uncertainties and doubts evaporate.

No longer is this moment about the dos and don’ts.

The are you making good choices and what is your next move.

No, now it’s about me. It’s about the now.

I feel invincible, untouchable as the chimes begin to fade.

The outside slowly creeps back in.

The voices invade.

The tension disappears.

In one swift moment I have left my own sphere.

I re-enter my reality.

Questions: A Replacement Poem

They told the children who live the meaning of life to say what is laughter.

And they saw the sky that demands the noise of thousands of children.

I bowed my head and stretched my arms as though I was trying to play with them.

And then one Friday night they wandered by the abandoned house.

And they encountered another child by the river with her sister and brother

and a baby doll and a letter.
